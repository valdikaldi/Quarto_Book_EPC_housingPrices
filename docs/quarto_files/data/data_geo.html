4.3 Geographical data

To control for surrounding characteristics of properties, i.e. neighbourhood characteristics, we construct variables from geographical data. We begin by constructing dummy variables characterising the area surrounding the properties, such as highways, train tracks, forests and the ocean. These dummy variables are constructed by creating a 100 meter buffer, i.e., a circle with a radius of 100 meters is created around the coordinates of properties, and if a point of an object of interest falls within the buffer zone, then the dummy variable gets the value 1 else 0. Note that the coordinates of the properties were obtained when scraping the Boliga (n.d.) website. Defining the area of properties, i.e., neighbourhoods can be a bit tricky as there is no consensus on the definition of what constitutes a neighbourhood in terms of meters or kilometres. Throughout this paper, we will use a 100-meter buffer size, and we will also use different buffer sizes as robustness tests. To create these dummy variables, we use the Geofabrik OSM dataset for Denmark, which is compiled data from OpenStreetMap (Geofabrik GmbH n.d.). Next, we calculate the road distance in minutes from the capital, i.e. Copenhagen, and the radius of the surface area from a 30-minute isochrone with a car of each property.

An isochrone represents a surface area which is reachable from a given point within a specific time threshold. The intuition of taking the radius from the isochrone area is to construct a simple proxy variable to represent accessibility. We took inspiration from Biazzo, Monechi, and Loreto (2019) when creating our accessibility variable. The accessibility variable used in this paper will therefore represent the radius of the surface area for each dwelling at a 30 minute time threshold with a car. In relation to this, Wahlström (2016) used an accessibility index to account for potential spatial auto-correlations, however, they compute their accessibility as the travel time between municipalities with respect to the population of municipality of destination.

To compute both our accessibility variable and the road travel time to Copenhagen in minutes for each dwelling, we utilised a docker image of the Valhalla Route Engine (Valhalla Developers n.d.). Valhalla, an open-source route engine, provides a range of tools for geographical data analysis, including the calculation of isochrones for points of interest and the travel time in minutes between two points (Valhalla Developers n.d.). In addition, we incorporate the average population density of Denmark at a 1km grid level. Eurostat provides access to population density at grid level for four census years: 2006, 2011, 2018 and 2021 [Eurostat (2023)}. We aggregate the data from all census years, resulting in our population density variable representing the average population density at a 1 km grid level over the specified period.

The reason it is so far is because it involves driving to the harbour in Rønne on Bornholm, taking a ferry with the car to a town called Køge, and then driving from Køge to Copenhagen.
